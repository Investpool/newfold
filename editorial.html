EDITORIAL

THE EYES OF GHANA ON THE TRUE VALUE OF ACCOUNTABILITY

From looting to recovery: Have you exhausted the Auditor-General’s 2024 report? If you have, then you know it is not a book of numbers, it is a record of suffering. GH¢18.4 billion, about US$1.47 billion at today’s Bank of Ghana rate, is not a statistic, it is a stolen future. It is the child who went to school without food, the patient who sat in pain because the medicine never came, the farmer who watched his harvest waste while politicians signed contracts in air-conditioned rooms. Each irregularity is not an error on paper, it is a wound carved into the lives of ordinary Ghanaians.


Take the Electricity Company of Ghana under Samuel Dubik Mahama. The auditors found ECG collected GH¢11.59 billion but declared GH¢8.63 billion. That is a gap of nearly GH¢3 billion. Imagine what that means: enough to supply every senior high school with free textbooks for years. Instead, the money slipped into the fog of under-declarations. ECG also withheld GH¢1.29 billion from the very power producers who keep the lights on. And when they did spend, it was GH¢75 million handed to Hubtel without even a contract, later dressed up in a retroactive single source deal never approved by the Public Procurement Authority Board. Ghost vehicles, phantom tablets, inflated conductor prices, the company that powers our homes was running on shadows.


And while all this was happening, families in Kasoa and Tamale sat through power cuts, children struggled over their homework with phone torches, and patients in hospitals were told to bring their own oxygen. That is what a GH¢2.95 billion under-declaration really looks like when you step outside the accounting books.


ECG’s rot does not end on paper. At Tema Port, an independent audit showed over 1,300 ECG containers unaccounted for. The Ministry of Energy, led by Dr. John Jinapor, set up a tracking committee, arrests were made, and later investigations traced about 2,637 containers linked to ECG. Yet not all the boxes are back in the ledger: dozens remained unaccounted for, and some retrieved had never been recorded in ECG’s books. By July 2025, the Energy Minister confirmed the missing count had dropped to 23. This is not closure; it is evidence of how easily national assets slip into the fog of weak records and porous oversight.


It was not only ECG. At the Bulk Oil Storage and Transportation Company (BOST), contracts were signed outside the mandatory e-procurement system, projects stalled, and devices bought with public money gathered dust. The Minerals Income Investment Fund (MIIF), under Edward Nana Yaw Koranteng, advanced US$3.8 million, seventy percent, on a US$5.42 million contract, flouting the Public Financial Management Act which caps such advances at fifteen percent. The National Lottery Authority under Samuel Awuku withheld GH¢1.44 million in taxes in 2022 and GH¢3.28 million in 2023, never remitted to the Ghana Revenue Authority. The State Interests and Governance Authority under Edward Boateng failed to collect GH¢749,000 in rent arrears from state enterprises. Even the Ghana Library Authority, custodian of learning, sat on over a million cedis in withholding taxes between 2019 and 2023.


And the culprits are not faceless. They were CEOs, Directors, Ministers, and regulators. They drove past children hawking at traffic lights, knowing their decisions had stripped the state of the very resources that could have changed those children’s lives. They sat in air-conditioned conference rooms and signed away public funds while the rest of the country queued at NHIS offices, waited at ECG prepaid meters, or begged for boreholes in rural villages.


And yet even this is not the full picture. There are red flags not captured in the Auditor-General’s report, irregularities that never made it onto the printed pages. From sweetheart contracts in obscure agencies to unexplained transfers buried in classified accounts, the questions multiply. If GH¢18.4 billion is what was found, how much more lies hidden in the shadows?


Today, there is a new government. President John Mahama has launched Operation Recover All Loot. Its committee, chaired by Samuel Okudzeto Ablakwa, reviewed 36 high-profile cases valued at over US$20 billion and handed its dossier to the Attorney General. National Security has already made arrests in the ECG container scandal. The Office of the Special Prosecutor is probing a GH¢280 million fraud at the National Petroleum Authority. EOCO has reopened the Cecilia Dapaah docket. Cases from mining, procurement, and financial institutions are now before prosecutors.


But the real test is not in speeches. It is in the mother in Techiman who pays VAT on a tin of milk, expecting that her money will reach the state. It is in the teacher in Hohoe who waits for chalk, only to be told there is no money. It is in the farmer in Sefwi who applied for a COCOBOD seed fund loan and was told the coffers were empty. They are the true auditors of Ghana, and they know the difference between promises and prosecutions.


Even the shadows cannot hide this reality: Ghana is not poor. Ghana is being plundered through paperwork. And the victims are not in Parliament or boardrooms; they are in the villages, markets, and hospital corridors.


The Auditor-General has spoken. The law has been triggered. Now the people wait to see whether Operation Recover All Loot will remain a slogan or become a scalpel. Because if GH¢18.4 billion can vanish in one year while children sit in darkness, then every Ghanaian is owed not just an audit, but justice.


This time, we cannot afford another report gathering dust. This time, we must see names in court, assets seized, accounts frozen, and handcuffs clinking louder than excuses.


Kay Codjoe
